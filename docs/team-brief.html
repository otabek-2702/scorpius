Scorpius — Team Brief

Build with AI: EdTech Hackathon · 23–24 May 2026 · New Uzbekistan University

Read this once before the hackathon. It tells you what we're building, what you personally do, and how the two days run. Most of us haven't done a hackathon before — that's fine. This is the map.

1. What we're building — Scorpius

Scorpius is an AI tutor that turns emaktab.uz from a passive gradebook into a personalized tutor. It reads a student's real grades, finds the exact lesson in the Uzbek curriculum, and teaches it one swipeable card at a time — then briefs the parent. Our track is General Education (schools, learners, parents, teachers).

Why it can win: it's built on real emaktab data nobody else has, and a genuine adaptive learning engine — not a thin chatbot. The judges explicitly reward AI that "supports the core value," not a chatbot layer.

2. The hackathon — the hard facts

  • Two-day build, 23–24 May, New Uzbekistan University. Venue closes 19:00 each day (remote work allowed after, no official support).
  • Platform: ai.gdgtashkent.uz — team registration, dashboard, checkpoint status, final submission.
  • Every member must register individually on the GDG portal BEFORE the event starts. After it starts, only the team lead registers the team.
  • Final submission: 24 May, 14:30 sharp — the dashboard locks at the deadline. Two links required: a Google Slides deck (official template, shared "Anyone with the link – Viewer") + a public GitHub repo with a clear README.
  • Presentation: 6 minutes — 4 min pitch + demo, 1 min judge Q&A, 1 min scoring. A working demo beats long slides.

7. For first-time hackathoners — how to be useful

  • A hackathon is a sprint: a real problem turned into a working demo in ~30 hours. A demo that works beats a beautiful slide that doesn't.
  • If you're not writing code, your job is just as real: become the expert on the problem — why Uzbek students and parents need this, with concrete evidence — and turn that into the Slides and the pitch story.
  • Be at the table during checkpoint windows. Be reachable. Ask mentors questions — that is allowed and smart.
  • Small, steady help compounds: test the app, find one good statistic, prep one slide, catch one bug in the story. Don't disappear.

8. One honest rules note — read this

The rules say the project must "start at the hackathon" and that "pre-built full solutions are not allowed." We have early scaffolding and the design done as preparation. Two things keep us safe:

  • Before CP1, Amirsaid asks an organizer/mentor directly what is acceptable, given we are continuing our Ideathon idea — get a clear answer in person.
  • Disclose everything — every tool, library, template, AI agent, and all prior work — in the README and Slides. The rules require disclosure; judges review the repository and its history. Transparency protects us.

There is still a large amount to build live during the 34 hours — that is genuine hackathon work, done openly.
